How to use this data

All certificates uploaded to the Energy Performance of Buildings Register are shown. Some certificates that are shown may have expired or been replaced.

Energy certificates produced in different years are not directly comparable due to changes in methodology.

Not all buildings in the UK have an energy certificate.

Energy certificates are not a direct indicator of housing supply or the state of the housing or rental market.

This data is not classed as official statistics. Find out what this means.

This data is updated monthly and covers:

  • England
  • Wales
  • Northern Ireland

For data in Scotland, visit the Scottish Energy Performance Certificate Register.

What does this mean?
Energy performance for new buildings and new conversions is calculated using the SAP methodology. It is calculated by an assessor with access to the building specifications.

Energy performance for existing buildings is calculated using the RdSAP methodology. It is calculated by assuming certain building specifications based on the year that the building was constructed.

Energy efficiency rating

What does this mean?
Energy efficiency rating (EER) is a metric based on how much it costs to heat and light a home to an agreed standard. It is calculated as a numerical score that typically ranges between 1 (high cost) and 100 (low cost).

The numerical score can be presented as a letter range between A and G as shown below:

A rating – 92 plus

B rating – 81 to 91

C rating – 69 to 80

D rating – 55 to 68

E rating – 39 to 54

F rating – 21 to 38

G rating – 1 to 20

What does this mean?

Commercial energy performance certificates are for private commercial buildings. They are based on the carbon dioxide emissions of the building.

Display energy certificates are for public buildings. They are based on the energy use of the building.

Air conditioning inspection certificates are for buildings that contain air conditioning systems above 12 kW.
